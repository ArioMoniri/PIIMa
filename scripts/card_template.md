## The three headline numbers

They are reported separately and are never blended. A single score covering all three would
let a strong result on one hide a breach in another.

### 1. Direct-identifier recall

A missed direct identifier is a breach, so recall is the number that matters and precision is
a feature. Recall is reported per entity type as well as in aggregate, because an aggregate
figure can sit comfortably on top of one weak entity class.

### 2. Medical-term false-positive rate

Clinical text in this language is saturated with Latin and English medical vocabulary, often
carrying local morphology. Masking a diagnosis, a drug or an anatomical term destroys the
note, so legitimate medical vocabulary forms an explicit negative set and is scored as a
false-positive rate rather than folded into precision.

### 3. Contextual re-identification rate

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|

| Validated by | `{{ contextual_validated_by }}` |

Contextual quasi-identifiers are validated by an adversarial re-identification red team and
are deliberately not scored by F1, because narrative re-identification has no clean ground
truth: two competent annotators legitimately disagree about whether a given phrase
re-identifies a patient, so any F1 over those spans would be a measurement of annotation habit
rather than of privacy. The red-team rate is the authoritative figure. Where it reads
`not measured`, the contextual tier is unvalidated on this run: the absence of an attack is
not a survived attack, and the coverage figure is not a substitute for one.

## Intended use

- De-identifying clinical free text in `{{ language_primary }}` before it is shared, archived,
  or sent to a system that must not receive identifiers.
- Producing a span map for reversible masking inside a controlled environment.
- Benchmarking against `{{ dataset_name }}`.

## Out of scope

- Structured identifier fields in a hospital information system. Use the source system's own
  controls; this model reads prose.
- Languages other than `{{ language | join(", ") }}`. Numbers measured here do not transfer to
  another language, and a card that reuses them for one would be reporting a different
  evaluation than the one it names.
- Any deployment where an unreviewed automatic decision is the final control. De-identification
  output should be reviewed under the compliance regime that governs the data.

## Limitations

- **Sight-unseen recall drops.** The measured drop on a held-out note type is
  {{ sight_unseen_recall_drop }}. This is expected rather than anomalous: published English
  systems lose several points of F1 on note types absent from training, and this model will
  too. If you deploy it on a document genre outside the evaluated corpus, assume recall is
  lower than the headline figure until you have measured it on your own material.
- **Recall is bounded by the gold set.** Every figure above is measured against a synthetic
  benchmark. A synthetic corpus can be adversarial and can be honest about what it contains,
  but it cannot prove behaviour on identifier surface forms nobody thought to write down.
- **Contextual quasi-identifiers are the hard part.** Employment references, relationships,
  assets and distinctive events re-identify patients without containing an entity a token
  classifier can tag. They are addressed only under the Expert Determination tier and are
  validated adversarially, not by F1.
- **Aggressive contextual masking trades readability for privacy.** The Expert Determination
  tier removes narrative detail that clinicians may consider clinically relevant. That
  tradeoff is a deployment decision, not a defect.
- **Masking is not anonymisation on its own.** Re-identification risk depends on the
  population and on what else the recipient holds. A small population makes weak
  quasi-identifiers strong.

## Evaluation methodology

Scoring is performed by the harness in `eval/harness.py` against quote-anchored gold fixtures
resolved to UTF-8 byte offsets. Gates come from `eval/thresholds.yaml`; the harness carries no
default thresholds of its own, so a missing gate fails a run rather than relaxing it. The
results artifact linked above is the complete record for this card, including every gate
verdict and per-entity count reproduced on this page.

To reproduce:

```bash
git checkout {{ eval_sha }}
python3 eval/run.py --detector {{ detector_name }} --gates
python3 eval/report.py {{ eval_run_path }} --gates
```

## Prior art

- Stubbs, Kotfila and Uzuner (2015), *Automated systems for the de-identification of
  longitudinal clinical narratives: Overview of 2014 i2b2/UTHealth shared task Track 1*,
  Journal of Biomedical Informatics. Source of the accepted micro-F1 bar used as a gate here.
- The i2b2/UTHealth and n2c2 de-identification tracks, which established the task definition
  and the evaluation conventions this benchmark follows.
- OpenMed NER (arXiv:2508.01630), a large open corpus of biomedical NER checkpoints. Its
  breadth is real work and is cited as such. This project differs in process rather than in
  ambition: it publishes one evaluation per published model, in the language of that model,
  and gates publication on that evaluation.
- TEHR (*Applied Sciences*, 2026), which reports that non-English clinical de-identification
  is underrepresented and that base models score poorly on this language's clinical NER before
  fine-tuning.
